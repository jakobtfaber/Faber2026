\subsection{Scattering index and burst multiplicity}
\label{sec:results-alpha}

% Demonstrated multi-component case = burst internally labelled "zach"
% (swap to its TNS name). Numbers from the matched-normalization component grid.
A subset of our co-detected bursts is resolved into more than one temporal
component \citep{Pleunis2021}, and in these cases the inferred scattering index
depends on whether that multiplicity is modeled. We illustrate this with one co-detection whose
DSA-110 profile shows two sub-pulses while its CHIME/FRB profile presents a
single prominent peak yet fails the residual-whiteness gate. On the
matched-normalization grid, adding a second CHIME/FRB component is favored by
$\Delta\ln Z\approx+3.6\times10^{3}$ over the single-component model---roughly
forty times the evidence gain of adding a second DSA-110 component---and places
a broad companion $0.34$\,ms behind the main peak, blended beneath the
scattering tail where a simple peak search does not resolve it. Including this
component shifts the shared scattering index from $\alpha=3.32\pm0.01$ to
$\alpha=2.76\pm0.02$ and raises the scattering time from
$\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}=0.42$ to $0.56$\,ms. The second DSA-110 sub-pulse, which
a single-band fit leaves pinned at the minimum-separation prior bound, relocates
to its observed delay only once the CHIME/FRB component constrains the shared
$(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\alpha)$: the two bands are coupled through one
scattering law.
