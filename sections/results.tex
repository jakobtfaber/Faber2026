\subsection{The per-sightline dispersion and scattering budget}
\label{sec:results-budget}
\label{sec:budget}

Applying the decomposition of Section~\ref{sec:dm} (Eq.~\ref{eq:dmbudget}) to the
twelve sightlines yields the budget of Table~\ref{tab:budget} and
Figure~\ref{fig:budget}. The Galactic term is modest and similar across the
sample ($\mathrm{DM_{MW}}=78$--$171\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$), while the
cosmological mean grows with redshift to dominate the higher-$z$ sightlines.
Confirmed foreground systems contribute a modeled circumgalactic column on
eight sightlines ($\mathrm{DM_{int}}\approx10$--$241\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$),
largest for FRB 20230307A, whose sightline threads six intersecting foreground
halos and the one confirmed $R_{500}$-piercing cluster
(Section~\ref{sec:dominant-systems}). One sightline (FRB 20230814B) passes
through a foreground galaxy's interior ($b\approx4.6$\,kpc,
$b<0.1\,R_{\mathrm{vir}}$), where $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ is capped at the
$b=0.1\,R_{\mathrm{vir}}$ value. Two sightlines (FRB 20220506D,
FRB 20240122A) return no foreground system in the applicable catalogs but lie
outside the deep-imaging survey footprints, so their intervening term is
unconstrained by the searched surveys rather than excluded
(Table~\ref{tab:budget}). The host contribution is the residual after the
modeled terms are removed; for the two highest-redshift sightlines
(FRB 20220310F, FRB 20221203A) it is formally negative---the modeled terms
over-predict $\mathrm{DM_{obs}}$. Both budgets are dominated by the
cosmological mean, so the negative residual bounds the sightline-to-sightline
scatter about the Macquart relation rather than the host, and a
prior-predictive sensitivity analysis quantifies the probability of a negative
host term per sightline. Three sightlines (FRB 20230325A, FRB 20240122A, FRB 20230814B) lack a
spectroscopic host redshift and are carried only through the
redshift-independent Galactic term.

The predicted intervening scattering is small everywhere: it spans
$\tau_{\mathrm{int}}\sim0.0005$--$0.024\,\mathrm{ms}$ at $1\,\mathrm{GHz}$ on
the eight sightlines carrying a modeled column (Figure~\ref{fig:budget},
right). The direct measured-versus-predicted comparison draws on the uniform
$\beta$-based re-fit of Section~\ref{sec:results-alpha}: Table~\ref{tab:beta}
supplies a citable $\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}$ for eleven of the twelve
sightlines (FRB 20240203A is withheld by the joint-fit quality gate), and
Figure~\ref{fig:budget} overlays each measurement on its predicted intervening
contribution. On ten of the eleven measured sightlines the predicted
intervening $\tau$ falls $1.5$--$3$ orders of magnitude below the measurement,
so the observed pulse broadening must arise in the host (or, for the
coverage-unconstrained pair, an unsearched intervening system). The single
exception is FRB 20230913A, where the foreground-halo prediction reaches
$\approx0.7$ of the measured value: the one sightline on which an identified
intervening galaxy plausibly dominates the observed scattering
(Section~\ref{sec:results-scintillation}).

\subsubsection{The dominant intervening systems}
\label{sec:dominant-systems}

Of the confirmed foreground census, eight sightlines carry a modeled
intervening column, but most contributions are modest
($\mathrm{DM_{int}}\approx10$--$84\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$;
Table~\ref{tab:budget}). Figure~\ref{fig:galaxies_cgm} characterizes the
dominant foreground galaxy along each of the three largest-column sightlines:
for each, the projected hot modified-NFW dispersion column
\citep{ProchaskaZheng2019} is shown as a function of impact parameter, with
the sightline's actual impact, the $R_{\mathrm{vir}}$ truncation, and the
$b=0.1\,R_{\mathrm{vir}}$ floor at which the smooth column is capped before it
is extrapolated through the galaxy interior. The largest total column is
FRB~20230307A's $\mathrm{DM_{int}}=241\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$, assembled from
six intersecting foreground halos plus the one confirmed $R_{500}$-piercing
cluster (below) rather than any single system---its dominant galaxy halo is a
$z=0.11$ grazer at $b\approx102$\,kpc contributing only
$14\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$. FRB~20221203A carries
$84\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$, dominated by a $z=0.46$ halo at $b\approx32$\,kpc
($b/R_{\mathrm{vir}}=0.27$). FRB~20230814B hosts the sample's only
galaxy-interior crossing: a $z=0.77$ halo pierced at $b\approx4.6$\,kpc
($b/R_{\mathrm{vir}}=0.04$), whose capped column is
$63\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$---with the caveat that this burst lacks a
spectroscopic host redshift, so the halo's foreground status is provisional
against the placeholder. Halo masses on all three sightlines are assumed
($L_\star$-scaled); the largest \emph{measured}-mass column is
FRB~20221113A's $z=0.047$ GLADE+ halo, grazing at $b\approx16$\,kpc
($b/R_{\mathrm{vir}}=0.14$) for $41\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$. Each panel plots
the dominant halo, so its annotated dispersion and pulse-broadening are that
halo's contribution alone; the per-sightline $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ and
$\tau_{\mathrm{int}}$ of Table~\ref{tab:budget} sum every confirmed foreground
system along the same sightline.

Three sightlines carry spectroscopically-confirmed foreground clusters
(Table~\ref{tab:foreground}), but FRB~20230307A is the only one whose sightline
pierces a cluster within $R_{500}$. That field hosts eight confirmed clusters, of
which Figure~\ref{fig:clusters_icm} characterizes the four innermost with the
FRB modified-NFW hot-baryon column, converting the catalog $M_{500}$ to an
approximate $M_{200}$. Only the nearest,
J115120.4+714435 ($z=0.200$, $\log(M_{500}/M_\odot)\approx14.1$), lies inside
its own $R_{500}$ ($b/R_{500}=0.83$), where the modified-NFW column gives
$\mathrm{DM}_{\rm cl}\approx160\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$; the next,
J115128.2+713637, passes just outside $R_{500}$ at $1.25\,R_{500}$
($\approx95\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$), and the remaining two cross at
$3$--$3.3\,R_{500}$, beyond the adopted virial truncation. The innermost
cluster contribution is folded into the intervening budget as a foreground-cluster
term; because the FRB~20230307A host residual remains small and positive after the
foreground subtraction, the cluster column should be read as a plausible budget
component rather than a unique attribution.

\subsection{Scintillation and screen attribution}
\label{sec:results-scintillation}

Figure~\ref{fig:scint_screens} shows the three separated scattering scales for the
prototype sightline FRB~20221203A. Extending the Milky-Way-floor comparison to all
twelve co-detections, the diffractive-scintillation excess relative to the modeled
Milky-Way floor is robust to the choice of Galactic
electron-density model among the scattering-calibrated models of the same lineage:
NE2025 \citep{Ocker2026} yields an excess on all six mid-latitude sightlines
($2.6$--$10.7\times$) and NE2001 \citep{CordesLazio2002} confirms it ($6/6$,
$2.2$--$7.3\times$), while YMW16 \citep{YMW2017} reverses it, predicting a floor below the
measured value on five of the six. We attribute this reversal to YMW16's per-sightline
scattering unreliability rather than to an absence of excess: by construction YMW16
incorporates no interstellar-scattering data and derives $\tau$ from an empirical
$\tau$--$\mathrm{DM}$ relation with $\sim\!1$\,dex scatter, whereas NE2025 and NE2001
forward-model scattering from the electron-density-fluctuation field. Because the adopted
NE2025 floor itself carries a factor $\sim\!2$--$3$ per-sightline uncertainty that acts as a
systematic shared across sightlines---and therefore does not average down as $\sqrt{N}$---we
regard only the high-amplitude sightlines ($\sim\!7$--$11\times$) as robust evidence for an
extragalactic scattering screen, with the lowest-amplitude cases ($\sim\!2.6$--$3\times$)
remaining consistent with the floor uncertainty.

  \label{fig:scint_screens}

The three-scale separation of Figure~\ref{fig:scint_screens} generalizes to a
sample-wide consistency test. For every sightline with a resolved DSA-band
decorrelation bandwidth we form the single-screen product
$\tau(1.4\,\mathrm{GHz})\,\Delta\nu_d(1.4\,\mathrm{GHz})$, taking $\tau$ from
the uniform joint re-fit (Table~\ref{tab:beta}) and $\Delta\nu_d$ from the
per-sub-band autocorrelation fits scaled to a common $1.4\,\mathrm{GHz}$ with
each burst's own fitted index; if one screen produces both the pulse
broadening and the scintillation, this product must lie between
$1/(2\pi)\approx0.16$ (thin screen) and $\approx1$ (extended medium)
\citep{Cordes2025}. Exactly one sightline satisfies it: FRB~20230913A
($\tau\,\Delta\nu_d=1.6$, within a factor of two of the extended-medium
value)---the same burst whose foreground halo supplies $\approx0.7$ of its
measured $\tau$ (Section~\ref{sec:results-budget}). Every other sightline
exceeds the single-screen range by a factor of $\sim$5 to
$\sim4\times10^{3}$ (FRB~20230325A is marginal at $2.6$), which means the
resolved diffractive scintillation and the pulse broadening arise on
\emph{different} screens, the scintillation screen being the nearer of the
two---the FRB~20221203A pattern of Figure~\ref{fig:scint_screens}, now seen
to be generic across the co-detected sample. The CHIME band lacks stored
sub-channel decorrelation fits, so this test is DSA-band only.

\subsection{Turbulence spectrum and burst multiplicity}
\label{sec:results-alpha}

Across the twelve co-detected sightlines, the uniform $\beta$-based joint fit
(Section~\ref{sec:jointfit}) was run to completion on every burst, with the
per-band component multiplicities (the $C\times D$ column of
Table~\ref{tab:beta}) locked per sightline from the fixed-s² evidence grids
where those are decisive (FRB~20220506D, FRB~20221113A, and FRB~20230814B at
C2D1; FRB~20220310F at C2D2), and at the adopted campaign choice where the
grid preference is weak (FRB~20240122A, fit at C1D1) or not robust between
adjacent counts (FRB~20230307A, fit at C3D3); every fit is verified against
its posterior-predictive check. Table~\ref{tab:beta} reports the eleven
citable sightlines; FRB~20240203A is excluded by the joint-fit quality gate
(catastrophic per-band misfit). The census divides cleanly in the continuous
$\beta$ parameter space, where the Kolmogorov value $\beta=11/3$
($\alpha\approx4.4$) and the square-law limit $\beta=4$ ($\alpha=4$,
exponential PBF) are specific points. Two sightlines return interior
posteriors---genuine power-law-tail members: FRB~20230325A
($\beta=3.72\pm0.01$, $\alpha=4.32$, just above the Kolmogorov point) and
FRB~20230307A ($\beta=3.23\pm0.02$, $\alpha=5.26$, the steepest frequency
scaling in the sample). The remaining nine posteriors rail at the $\beta=4$
bound: these sightlines are consistent with square-law statistics and an
exponential PBF, and we quote $\alpha=4$ for them only as a
geometry-conditioned limit under the thin-screen closure
(Table~\ref{tab:beta}, note d), not as a measurement---an extended-medium PBF
family may fit them without railing, and that re-analysis is deferred. No
sightline rails to the steep ($\beta=3$, $\alpha=6$) bound. Three of the
railed rows carry an elevated single-band $\chi^2_\nu$ (FRB~20221203A,
FRB~20230913A, FRB~20220207C) and are provisional pending a per-band
systematics pass; for FRB~20230913A in particular the single CHIME component
under-models its multi-peaked profile ($\chi^2_\nu=3.96$), which resolves the
earlier component-association ambiguity at the cost of that residual.

A subset of our co-detected bursts resolves into more than one temporal
component \citep{Pleunis2021}, and in these cases the inferred turbulence
spectrum depends on whether that multiplicity is modeled. The clearest case is
FRB~20220310F, whose DSA-110 profile resolves into two narrow sub-pulses
$0.34$\,ms apart while its CHIME/FRB profile is a broad, multi-peaked burst
that fails the residual-whiteness gate. A fit that models only one DSA-110
component cannot place a single scattered pulse under both sub-pulses and the
broad CHIME burst at once, and the shared $\beta$ rails against a prior
bound, leaving strong correlated residuals at both sub-pulse times
(Figure~\ref{fig:whitney_mult}, top; DSA whitened $\chi^2_\nu=16.1$). Adding
the second DSA-110 component is favored decisively and, critically,
\emph{stably}: on the matched-normalization grid the evidence gain is
$\Delta\ln Z\approx+2.7\times10^{3}$ and flat across the gain-prior variance
($+2706/+2683/+2671$ at $s^2=1/10/100$), so the component is real rather than
an artifact of the prior. With the second component in place, the fit unrails
to a well-constrained $\beta$ with white residuals in both bands
(Figure~\ref{fig:whitney_mult}, bottom; $\chi^2_\nu=1.1$ and $1.5$). The
inferred turbulence spectrum for this burst depends strongly on the
multiplicity: a missing sub-component drives $\beta$ to a prior rail,
distorting both the fitted PBF shape and $\alpha$ simultaneously. This
sensitivity does not afflict the well-constrained single-screen sightlines,
whose $\beta$ shift by $\le0.1$ in derived $\alpha$ under the same choices,
but it makes plain that multiplicity must be fixed before a marginal burst's
turbulence spectrum is quoted.

\subsection{Isotropic-equivalent energies}
\label{sec:burst-energies}

Applying the band-restricted energy calculation of
Section~\ref{sec:methods-energies}, the eight sightlines with a spectroscopic
host redshift and a quality-passing joint fit span
$E_{\mathrm{iso}}=5\times10^{38}$--$1\times10^{41}\,\mathrm{erg}$
(Table~\ref{tab:burst-energies}). Bursts whose hosts lack a spectroscopic
redshift or for which no joint $c_0,\gamma$ fit exists are omitted
(Table~\ref{tab:burst-energies} caption). The most distant sightline,
FRB~20221203A ($z=0.51$), reaches
$E_{\mathrm{iso}}\simeq1.1\times10^{41}\,\mathrm{erg}$, at the luminous end of
the co-detected sample. The absolute flux scale is anchored to the DSA-110
catalog \citep{Law2024} for FRB~20220207C, which reproduces the catalog value to
within a factor of two; the remaining bands rely on the radiometer model without
an independent catalog cross-check, so their energies carry the additional,
unquantified systematic of the absolute scale. Because Eq.~\ref{eq:eiso} omits
the unobserved $0.8$--$1.3\,\mathrm{GHz}$ gap, the tabulated values are lower
limits on the total $0.4$--$1.5\,\mathrm{GHz}$ energy.
