\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The dispersion measure (DM) of a fast radio burst (FRB) is an integral of free
electrons along the line of sight, and as such a sum of contributions from
physically distinct media: the Milky-Way interstellar medium and halo, the
intergalactic medium whose mean grows with redshift through the Macquart
relation \citep{Macquart2020}, the circumgalactic medium of any intervening
galaxy the sightline pierces, and the FRB's host galaxy
\citep{CordesChatterjee2019}. The cosmological term makes the FRB DM a baryon
probe, but only once the foreground and host contributions are separated;
conversely, an intervening galaxy's circumgalactic column is itself a probe of
cool halo gas, accessible only where it can be isolated from the other terms.

Scattering carries complementary information. Multipath propagation through
turbulent ionized gas broadens the pulse by a time
$\tau(\nu)=\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}\,(\nu/1\,\mathrm{GHz})^{-\alpha}$, whose
frequency-scaling index $\alpha$ is determined by the same turbulence spectrum
that sets the pulse-broadening function (PBF) shape: an electron-density
fluctuation spectrum $P_n(q)\propto q^{-\beta}$ yields both a PBF shape derived
from the phase structure function $D_\phi(\rho)\propto\rho^{\beta-2}$ and a
frequency scaling $\alpha=2\beta/(\beta-2)$ for a thin screen
\citep{Rickett1990, Cordes2025}. A Kolmogorov spectrum ($\beta=11/3$) gives
$\alpha\simeq4.4$ and a power-law PBF tail; square-law statistics ($\beta=4$)
give $\alpha=4$ and an exponential PBF. Measured pulse-broadening indices
frequently fall below the Kolmogorov value \citep{Bhat2004}, but because
$\beta$---not $\alpha$---is the fundamental turbulence parameter, interpreting
such deviations requires fitting $\beta$ directly so that the PBF shape and
frequency scaling remain mutually consistent. Because $\tau$ and its frequency
scaling enter a single observing band only through the broadening at that band's
frequency, a single-band measurement cannot separate them: the same data are
reproduced by a large $\tau$ with a steep scaling or a small $\tau$ with a
shallow one.

Simultaneous detection across a wide frequency baseline breaks this degeneracy.
The twelve FRBs studied here were each recorded by both CHIME/FRB at
$0.4$--$0.8$\,GHz \citep{CHIMEFRB2018} and DSA-110 at $\sim$1.4\,GHz
\citep{Law2024}, a $\sim$1\,GHz lever arm over which the ratio of the two
scattering times fixes the frequency scaling directly. We use this leverage to
ask, sightline by sightline, which medium contributes which observed
feature---decomposing each burst's DM into its Galactic, cosmological,
intervening, and host terms (Section~\ref{sec:budget}), attributing its
scattering to a Galactic, intervening, or host screen
(Section~\ref{sec:scattering}), and fitting the turbulence spectral index
$\beta$ from a joint two-band model in which the PBF shape and $\alpha$ are
both derived from $\beta$ at each likelihood evaluation
(Section~\ref{sec:jointfit}). Section~\ref{sec:obs} describes the sample,
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the per-sightline budget and the
isotropic-equivalent energies, and Sections~\ref{sec:discussion}--%
\ref{sec:conclusions} discuss the cases where an intervening galaxy is the likely
scattering screen and what co-detection adds.
