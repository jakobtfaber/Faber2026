\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The dispersion measure (DM) of a fast radio burst (FRB) is an integral of free
electrons along the line of sight, and as such a sum of contributions from
physically distinct media: the Milky-Way interstellar medium and halo, the
intergalactic medium whose mean grows with redshift through the Macquart
relation \citep{Macquart2020}, the circumgalactic medium of any intervening
galaxy the sightline pierces, and the FRB's host galaxy
\citep{CordesChatterjee2019}. The cosmological term makes the FRB DM a baryon
probe, but only once the foreground and host contributions are separated;
conversely, an intervening galaxy's circumgalactic column is itself a probe of
cool halo gas, accessible only where it can be isolated from the other terms.

Scattering carries complementary information. Multipath propagation through
turbulent ionized gas broadens the pulse by a time
$\tau(\nu)=\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}\,(\nu/1\,\mathrm{GHz})^{-\alpha}$, whose
spectral index $\alpha$ encodes the turbulent density spectrum and the
scattering geometry: a Kolmogorov thin screen predicts $\alpha\simeq4.4$
\citep{Rickett1990}, while measured pulse-broadening indices frequently fall
below this value \citep{Bhat2004}. Because $\tau$ and $\alpha$ enter a single
observing band only through the broadening at that band's frequency, a
single-band measurement cannot separate them: the same data are reproduced by a
large $\tau$ with a steep index or a small $\tau$ with a shallow one.

Simultaneous detection across a wide frequency baseline breaks this degeneracy.
The twelve FRBs studied here were each recorded by both CHIME/FRB at
$0.4$--$0.8$\,GHz \citep{CHIMEFRB2018} and DSA-110 at $\sim$1.4\,GHz
\citep{Law2024}, a $\sim$1\,GHz lever arm over which the ratio of the two
scattering times fixes $\alpha$ directly. We use this leverage to ask, sightline
by sightline, which medium contributes which observed feature---decomposing each
burst's DM into its Galactic, cosmological, intervening, and host terms
(Section~\ref{sec:budget}), attributing its scattering to a Galactic,
intervening, or host screen (Section~\ref{sec:scattering}), and measuring the
shared $(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\alpha)$ with a joint two-band fit
(Section~\ref{sec:jointfit}). Section~\ref{sec:obs} describes the sample,
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the per-sightline budget and the
isotropic-equivalent energies, and Sections~\ref{sec:discussion}--%
\ref{sec:conclusions} discuss the cases where an intervening galaxy is the likely
scattering screen and what co-detection adds.
