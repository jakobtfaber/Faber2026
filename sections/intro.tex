\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Fast radio bursts (FRBs) provide compact, bright backlights through ionized gas
on Galactic, circumgalactic, intergalactic, and host-galaxy scales. Their
dispersion measures trace the integrated electron column, while pulse broadening
and diffractive scintillation respond to turbulent structure and screen geometry
\citep[e.g.,][]{CordesChatterjee2019}. Jointly interpreting these observables is
often degenerate: a given dispersion measure can be distributed among multiple
media, and a given scattering time can be produced by different screen
locations and turbulence spectra.

Co-detections by CHIME/FRB and DSA-110 help break these degeneracies by
measuring the same burst over a wide frequency baseline. CHIME/FRB samples the
lower-frequencies at 400---800~MHz, where dispersion delays and scattering are larger, while DSA-110 observes near 1.4~GHz, where the same event is less broadened. Combining the two bands provides direct constraints on the frequency-scaling of arrival-time structure and pulse broadening that are not available from either dataset alone. It also introduces a critical association step: the two detections must be demonstrated to be the same astrophysical burst before their dispersion and scattering information can be combined.

This paper is organized around that joint interpretation. We first define the
candidate co-detection sample and the data products used for each telescope. We
then describe the association test, the dispersion-budget model, the foreground
census used to identify potential intervening screens, the two-band scattering
model, the scintillation analysis, and the band-limited energy calculation. In
this working draft, the Results and Discussion outline the intended
measurements and the validation tests required for each one; numerical values
will be inserted once those analyses have passed internal consistency and
quality-control checks.
