\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Fast radio bursts (FRBs) provide compact, bright backlights through ionized gas
on Galactic, circumgalactic, intergalactic, and host-galaxy scales. Their
dispersion measures trace integrated electron column, while pulse broadening and
diffractive scintillation respond to turbulent structure and screen geometry
\citep[e.g.,][]{CordesChatterjee2019}. Interpreting those observables together is
powerful but underconstrained: the same observed dispersion measure can be
partitioned among several media, and the same scattering time can arise from
different screen locations and turbulence spectra.

Co-detections by CHIME/FRB and DSA-110 change that problem. CHIME/FRB samples
the lower-frequency part of the burst, where scattering and dispersion delays
are larger, while DSA-110 observes near 1.4 GHz, where the same event is less
broadened. The combined frequency baseline gives a direct lever arm on timing,
scattering, and frequency scaling that is not available from either band alone.
It also creates a stringent association problem: the two detections must be
shown to be the same astrophysical burst before their dispersion and scattering
information can be combined.

This paper is organized around that joint interpretation. We first define the
candidate co-detection sample and the data products used for each telescope. We
then describe the association test, the dispersion-budget model, the foreground
census needed to identify intervening screens, the two-band scattering model,
the scintillation connection, and the band-restricted energy calculation. The
Results and Discussion are deliberately scaffolded in this circulation draft:
they identify the result slots and validation dependencies, while withholding
numerical conclusions until the producing analyses pass the trust-restoration
checks.
