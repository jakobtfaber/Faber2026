\subsection{Dispersion Measure Decomposition}
\label{sec:dm}

The observed dispersion measure of each co-detection separates into Galactic,
cosmological, host, and (where the sightline intersects a foreground galaxy)
intervening contributions \citep{CordesChatterjee2019},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{DM_{obs}} = \mathrm{DM_{MW}} + \langle\mathrm{DM_{cosmic}}\rangle(z)
+ \mathrm{DM_{int}} + \mathrm{DM_{host}},
\label{eq:dmbudget}
\end{equation}
with the Galactic term $\mathrm{DM_{MW}} = \mathrm{DM_{MW,ISM}} +
\mathrm{DM_{MW,halo}}$ split between disk and halo. We estimate the disk
contribution $\mathrm{DM_{MW,ISM}}$ from the NE2001 \citep{CordesLazio2002} and
YMW16 \citep{YMW2017} electron-density models and adopt a
$\sim$40\,pc\,cm$^{-3}$ Milky-Way halo prior \citep{YamasakiTotani2020}. The
cosmological mean $\langle\mathrm{DM_{cosmic}}\rangle(z)$ follows the Macquart
relation \citep{Macquart2020}. For the sightlines that cross a foreground
galaxy we model the intervening circumgalactic column $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ as a
two-phase (hot modified-NFW plus clumpy cool) medium, taking the mNFW gas
profile from \citet{ProchaskaZheng2019}. Such intervening halos can imprint
measurable dispersion and, where the gas is turbulent, scattering on FRB
sightlines \citep{Ocker2021}, although an individual quiescent halo may instead
yield only an upper limit on its scattering contribution \citep{Prochaska2019}.
The residual after the modeled terms are removed is attributed to the host.

\subsection{Intervening Foreground Galaxies and Clusters}
\label{sec:foreground}

The intervening term $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ is sourced only where a sightline
actually intersects foreground circumgalactic or intracluster gas, so we first
establish which candidate systems along the twelve sightlines are genuinely in
the foreground. Each candidate is cross-matched against the DESI Legacy Imaging
Survey DR9 with \citet{Zhou2021} photometric redshifts, DESI DR1 spectroscopic
redshifts, the NASA/IPAC Extragalactic Database (NED), and PS1-STRM
\citep{Beck2021}---by coordinate match (morphology and redshift) and, for the
PS1-classified halos, by direct \texttt{objID} lookup. We label a candidate
\emph{confirmed} foreground when its best catalog redshift lies below the FRB
host redshift, \emph{background} when at or above it, and \emph{inconclusive}
when the redshift straddles the host within $1\sigma$, the host lacks a
spectroscopic redshift, or no trustworthy redshift exists (a PS1-STRM
\texttt{UNSURE} classification or extrapolated photo-$z$). Of the 49 candidates
in Table~\ref{tab:foreground}, 29 are confirmed foreground, 7 are background,
and 13 remain inconclusive; the photometric redshifts in the source compilation
are superseded by the catalog values reported here. Only one of the 15 clusters
has an impact parameter inside its own $R_{500}$, so for the rest the sightline
does not pierce the intracluster medium and the cluster contributes negligibly
to $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$.

\subsection{Scattering Attribution}
\label{sec:scattering}

We measure the pulse-broadening time and its frequency scaling from the
co-detected dynamic spectra and compare them against the scattering predicted
by each candidate screen: the Milky-Way ISM, an intervening circumgalactic
medium, or a host galaxy, where the host can dominate the scattering of a
heavily broadened burst \citep{Ocker2022}. The fits are produced by the
\texttt{dsa110-FLITS} pipeline (\texttt{pipeline/}).

\subsubsection{The joint two-band fit}
\label{sec:jointfit}
\label{sec:beta-scattering-methods}

A single-band scattering fit cannot separate the scattering timescale from its
frequency scaling, because the pulse broadening
$\tau(\nu)=\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}\,(\nu/1\,\mathrm{GHz})^{-\alpha}$ enters a
single band only through its value at that band's frequency: one band fixes the
product, not the slope. Co-detection breaks this degeneracy---the $\sim$1\,GHz
lever arm between CHIME/FRB ($\sim$0.4--0.8\,GHz) and DSA-110
($\sim$1.4\,GHz) measures the frequency scaling directly---but breaking the
degeneracy is not the same as measuring $\alpha$ in a physically consistent way.

The pulse-broadening function (PBF) shape and the scattering index $\alpha$ are
not independent observables. Both descend from the same turbulence spectrum: a
power-law electron-density fluctuation spectrum
$P_n(q)\propto q^{-\beta}$ produces a phase structure function
$D_\phi(\rho)\propto\rho^{\beta-2}$, whose Fourier transform determines the
angular brightness distribution and hence the PBF shape, while the diffractive
scale and Fresnel arguments give the frequency scaling
$\tau\propto\nu^{-2\beta/(\beta-2)}$ for a thin screen---i.e.\
$\alpha=2\beta/(\beta-2)$ \citep{Rickett1990, Cordes2025}. The common
one-sided exponential PBF,
$p_E(t,\tau)=\tau^{-1}e^{-t/\tau}\,\Theta(t)$, corresponds to a square-law
structure function $D_\phi(\rho)\propto\rho^2$ ($\beta=4$), for which
$\alpha=4$. A Kolmogorov spectrum ($\beta=11/3$) instead yields a
power-law PBF tail $p_d(t)\propto t^{-\beta/2}$ and $\alpha\approx4.4$
\citep{Cordes2025}.

Because $\beta$ determines both the PBF shape and $\alpha$, they must be
co-modeled. Choosing a fixed PBF family and freely varying $\alpha$ inside it
is physically inconsistent: the exponential PBF assumes square-law
statistics, which already imply $\alpha\approx4$. If the true medium is
Kolmogorov---or anything other than square-law---fitting an exponential PBF
will absorb the shape mismatch into a biased $\alpha$ \citep{Cordes2025}.

We therefore replace the phenomenological pair $(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\alpha)$
with the turbulence spectral index $\beta$ as the fundamental parameter. For
each $\beta$ in the fit, the PBF shape is computed directly from the
corresponding phase structure function $D_\phi(\rho)\propto\rho^{\beta-2}$, and
the frequency scaling index follows as $\alpha=2\beta/(\beta-2)$ under the
thin-screen assumption. The free parameters of the joint fit are
$(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\beta)$ and the per-telescope pulse parameters
(arrival time $t_0$, intrinsic width $\zeta$, and a residual dispersion
$\delta\mathrm{DM}$). The fit operates on the CHIME/FRB and DSA-110 dynamic
spectra simultaneously, with the PBF shape and $\alpha$ both derived from
$\beta$ at every likelihood evaluation rather than chosen independently.

Under this model, the Kolmogorov value $\beta=11/3$ ($\alpha\approx4.4$) and the
square-law limit $\beta=4$ ($\alpha=4$, exponential PBF) are specific points in
the physically allowed thin-screen inertial-range mapping. Because
$\alpha=2\beta/(\beta-2)$ decreases as $\beta$ increases in this regime, a
formally shallow empirical scaling is not interpreted by itself as a turbulence
spectral index; it instead indicates that the assumed scattering family, burst
morphology, or inertial-range closure is incomplete.

\subsubsection{Sub-band validation of the frequency scaling}
\label{sec:subband}
\label{sec:beta-subband}

The frequency scaling recovered by the joint fit can be validated independently
of the PBF parametrization by fitting the scattering time directly in frequency
sub-bands. We split each band into sub-bands (four across CHIME, three across
DSA-110), collapse each to a one-dimensional profile, and fit a scattered
pulse---a Gaussian intrinsic shape convolved with a one-sided exponential tail,
the exponentially-modified Gaussian---to each sub-band independently, leaving the
scattering time $\tau$ free at every frequency. Because each $\tau_i$ is measured
per sub-band with no power law imposed, the slope of $\tau_i(\nu_i)$ is an
estimate of the frequency scaling that shares neither parametrization nor
likelihood with the joint two-dimensional fit, so its agreement with the joint
value is an independent validation rather than a self-consistency check
(Figure~\ref{fig:subband_tau}). The sub-band traces follow a declining power
law in every band where $\tau$ is resolved, and their slopes reproduce the
retired free-$\alpha$ exponential-PBF joint fits they were designed to
cross-check (e.g.\ FRB~20240229A, sub-band $\alpha=2.40$ versus joint
$2.35\pm0.03$; FRB~20221203A, $2.56$ versus $2.45\pm0.07$). Under the
$\beta$-based co-model both of those sightlines rail at the square-law limit,
whose closure admits no $\alpha<4$
(Section~\ref{sec:disc-alpha}), so the shallow empirical sub-band slopes are
not read as turbulence indices: they are the same
exponential-parametrization scaling the retired model measured, and their
distance below the $\alpha=4$ closure floor quantifies the burst-morphology
and scattering-family incompleteness that the $\beta$ co-model instead
absorbs into a prior rail. The check also
localizes the one elevated band $\chi^2$ in our sample: the DSA profile of
FRB~20221203A is imperfectly fit, but its sub-band $\tau(\nu)$ still follow the
joint slope, so the residual reflects unmodeled profile sub-structure rather than a
failure of the single-screen scattering law. The method
has a built-in horizon: where a burst is faint and heavily scattered the per-sub-band
fit cannot resolve the broad CHIME tail, and at the top of the DSA band $\tau$ falls
below the time resolution, so the most strongly scattered sightlines (e.g.\
FRB~20220506D) retain only DSA detections over too short a lever to constrain
the scaling on their own---there the joint fit's cross-band lever does work that no
single band can. We therefore use the sub-band reconstruction as a corroborating
cross-check on the well-constrained sightlines, not as a primary estimator.

\subsubsection{Gain-marginalized likelihood}
\label{sec:gainmarg}
\label{sec:beta-gainmarg}

The per-channel amplitude of an FRB spectrum is unknown and strongly modulated
by scintillation; carried as a free spectral envelope it leaves correlated
structure in the fit residuals and invalidates a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test.
We therefore marginalize the per-channel gain analytically. With a Gaussian
prior on the gain the per-channel evidence is available in closed form (a
matched-filter likelihood), and the recovered gain absorbs both the burst
spectrum and the scintillation pattern. This removes the spectral and
scintillation structure from the residual, so that the reduced $\chi^2$ and the
lag-1 residual autocorrelation become meaningful quality gates, and it lowers
the sampled dimensionality. We
sample the posterior by nested sampling \citep{Skilling2004}, as implemented in
\texttt{dynesty} \citep{Speagle2020}, and quality-gate each fit on its reduced
$\chi^2$ and residual whiteness, treating $R^2$ and residual normality as
informational.

\subsubsection{Multiple temporal components}
\label{sec:multicomp}
\label{sec:beta-multicomp}

Some bursts comprise more than one temporal sub-component, a common feature of
the resolved FRB population \citep{Hessels2019, Pleunis2021}. An unmodeled
second component is partially absorbed by a single scattered pulse---as a steeper
frequency scaling and a larger scattering time---and so biases the inferred
$\beta$, distorting both the fitted PBF shape and $\alpha$ simultaneously. We
extend the likelihood to $N$ temporal components per band, each contributing its
own $(t_0,\zeta)$ while $(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\beta)$ remain shared across
both bands and all components, and we select the number of components per band
by Bayesian evidence. Because the evidence normalization differs between
likelihood variants, the component counts are compared on a single matched grid
in which even the one-component model is evaluated through the multi-component
likelihood, so that evidence differences are meaningful. Component arrival times
within a band are ordered and separated by a data-derived minimum spacing,
keeping the fit identifiable at every prior sample. A clean single-component
co-detection in our sample, for which the joint fit returns a well-constrained
$\beta$ with white residuals, validates the method; a multi-component case is
presented in Section~\ref{sec:results-alpha}.
