\subsection{Dispersion Measure Decomposition}
\label{sec:dm}

The observed dispersion measure of each co-detection separates into Galactic,
cosmological, host, and (where the sightline intersects a foreground galaxy)
intervening contributions \citep{CordesChatterjee2019},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{DM_{obs}} = \mathrm{DM_{MW}} + \langle\mathrm{DM_{cosmic}}\rangle(z)
+ \mathrm{DM_{int}} + \mathrm{DM_{host}},
\label{eq:dmbudget}
\end{equation}
with the Galactic term $\mathrm{DM_{MW}} = \mathrm{DM_{MW,ISM}} +
\mathrm{DM_{MW,halo}}$ split between disk and halo. We estimate the disk
contribution $\mathrm{DM_{MW,ISM}}$ from the NE2001 \citep{CordesLazio2002} and
YMW16 \citep{YMW2017} electron-density models and adopt a
$\sim$40\,pc\,cm$^{-3}$ Milky-Way halo prior \citep{YamasakiTotani2020}. The
cosmological mean $\langle\mathrm{DM_{cosmic}}\rangle(z)$ follows the Macquart
relation \citep{Macquart2020}. For the sightlines that cross a foreground
galaxy we model the intervening circumgalactic column $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ as a
two-phase (hot modified-NFW plus clumpy cool) medium, taking the mNFW gas
profile from \citet{ProchaskaZheng2019}. Such intervening halos can imprint
measurable dispersion and, where the gas is turbulent, scattering on FRB
sightlines \citep{Ocker2021}, although an individual quiescent halo may instead
yield only an upper limit on its scattering contribution \citep{Prochaska2019}.
The residual after the modeled terms are removed is attributed to the host.

\subsection{Intervening Foreground Galaxies and Clusters}
\label{sec:foreground}

The intervening term $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ is sourced only where a sightline
actually intersects foreground circumgalactic or intracluster gas, so we first
establish which candidate systems along the twelve sightlines are genuinely in
the foreground. Each candidate is cross-matched against the DESI Legacy Imaging
Survey DR9 with \citet{Zhou2021} photometric redshifts, DESI DR1 spectroscopic
redshifts, the NASA/IPAC Extragalactic Database (NED), and PS1-STRM
\citep{Beck2021}---by coordinate match (morphology and redshift) and, for the
PS1-classified halos, by direct \texttt{objID} lookup. We label a candidate
\emph{confirmed} foreground when its best catalog redshift lies below the FRB
host redshift, \emph{background} when at or above it, and \emph{inconclusive}
when the redshift straddles the host within $1\sigma$, the host lacks a
spectroscopic redshift, or no trustworthy redshift exists (a PS1-STRM
\texttt{UNSURE} classification or extrapolated photo-$z$). Of the 49 candidates
in Table~\ref{tab:foreground}, 29 are confirmed foreground, 7 are background,
and 13 remain inconclusive; the photometric redshifts in the source compilation
are superseded by the catalog values reported here. Only one of the 15 clusters
has an impact parameter inside its own $R_{500}$, so for the rest the sightline
does not pierce the intracluster medium and the cluster contributes negligibly
to $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$.

\subsection{Scattering Attribution}
\label{sec:scattering}

We measure the pulse-broadening time and its frequency scaling from the
co-detected dynamic spectra and compare them against the scattering predicted
by each candidate screen: the Milky-Way ISM, an intervening circumgalactic
medium, or a host galaxy, where the host can dominate the scattering of a
heavily broadened burst \citep{Ocker2022}. The fits are produced by the
\texttt{dsa110-FLITS} pipeline (\texttt{pipeline/}).

\subsubsection{The joint two-band fit}
\label{sec:jointfit}

A single-band scattering fit cannot separate the scattering index $\alpha$ from
the scattering time, because the pulse broadening
$\tau(\nu)=\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}\,(\nu/1\,\mathrm{GHz})^{-\alpha}$ enters a
single band only through its value at that band's frequency: one band fixes the
product, not the slope. Co-detection breaks this degeneracy. We fit the
CHIME/FRB ($\sim$0.4--0.8\,GHz) and DSA-110 ($\sim$1.4\,GHz) dynamic spectra
\emph{simultaneously} with a shared sightline pair
$(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\alpha)$ and per-telescope pulse parameters (arrival
time $t_0$, intrinsic width $\zeta$, and a residual dispersion
$\delta\mathrm{DM}$). The $\sim$1\,GHz lever arm between the bands measures
$\alpha$ directly: the ratio of the two scattering times pins the slope, while
the shared normalization $\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}$ pins its value at 1\,GHz. With
$\alpha$ held at the Kolmogorov value $4.4$ \citep{Rickett1990}, the two bands
instead return $\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}}$ estimates that disagree by up to an
order of magnitude for the same sightline---evidence that $\alpha\neq4.4$ along
these lines of sight.

\subsubsection{Gain-marginalized likelihood}
\label{sec:gainmarg}

The per-channel amplitude of an FRB spectrum is unknown and strongly modulated
by scintillation; carried as a free spectral envelope it leaves correlated
structure in the fit residuals and invalidates a $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test.
We therefore marginalize the per-channel gain analytically. With a Gaussian
prior on the gain the per-channel evidence is available in closed form (a
matched-filter likelihood), and the recovered gain absorbs both the burst
spectrum and the scintillation pattern. This removes the spectral and
scintillation structure from the residual, so that the reduced $\chi^2$ and the
lag-1 residual autocorrelation become meaningful quality gates, and it lowers
the sampled dimensionality. We
sample the posterior by nested sampling \citep{Skilling2004}, as implemented in
\texttt{dynesty} \citep{Speagle2020}, and quality-gate each fit on its reduced
$\chi^2$ and residual whiteness, treating $R^2$ and residual normality as
informational.

\subsubsection{Multiple temporal components}
\label{sec:multicomp}

Some bursts comprise more than one temporal sub-component, a common feature of
the resolved FRB population \citep{Hessels2019, Pleunis2021}. An unmodeled
second component is partially absorbed by a single scattered pulse---as a steeper slope
and a larger scattering time---and so biases the inferred $\alpha$. We extend
the likelihood to $N$ temporal components per band, each contributing its own
$(t_0,\zeta)$ while $(\tau_{1\,\mathrm{GHz}},\alpha)$ remain shared across both
bands and all components, and we select the number of components per band by
Bayesian evidence. Because the evidence normalization differs between likelihood
variants, the component counts are compared on a single matched grid in which
even the one-component model is evaluated through the multi-component
likelihood, so that evidence differences are meaningful. Component arrival times
within a band are ordered and separated by a data-derived minimum spacing,
keeping the fit identifiable at every prior sample. A clean single-component
co-detection in our sample, for which the joint fit returns
$\alpha=4.48\pm0.04$ with white residuals, validates the method; a
multi-component case is presented in Section~\ref{sec:results-alpha}.
