\section{Observations and Sample}
\label{sec:obs}

Our sample consists of candidate co-detections of fast radio bursts seen
by CHIME/FRB \citep{CHIMEFRB2018} at $0.4$--$0.8$\,GHz and by DSA-110
\citep{Law2024} at $\sim$1.4\,GHz between 2022 February and 2024 February. The
key advantage of the sample is that each candidate event is observed across a
wide frequency baseline, which---as developed in Section~\ref{sec:jointfit}---can
separate scattering time from frequency scaling more strongly than a single-band
fit. The twelve bursts, their TNS designations, sky positions,
detection epochs, and association diagnostics are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:sample}. The timing residuals in that table use the shared
DSA-DM reference convention; the per-telescope DM values and their CHIME--DSA
agreement are provided as a machine-readable provenance table in the archival
data release (Section~\ref{sec:data-availability}).

\subsection{Dynamic spectra and reduction}
\label{sec:data}

For each co-detection we analyze a pair of total-intensity dynamic spectra---one
per facility---derived from the CHIME/FRB and DSA-110 baseband recordings
(24 spectra in total; per-spectrum provenance and array geometry accompany the
archival data release, Section~\ref{sec:data-availability}). Each spectrum is
loaded with its frequency axis
standardized to ascending order and its per-channel bandpass normalized, so the
fitted amplitudes are free of the band-edge rolloff. All fits, quality gates,
and figures in this paper are produced with a common open-source reduction and
fitting pipeline, which we reference throughout for reproducibility. We do not
catalog the fine-scale baseband morphology of each burst here; the analysis
only depends on whether a burst resolves into more than one temporal component,
which is treated in the multi-component fit of
Section~\ref{sec:multicomp}.

\subsection{Milky Way foreground contributions}
\label{sec:obs-mw}

To characterize the expected propagation effects contributed by the Milky Way, we query the NE2025 Galactic electron density model \citep{Ocker2026} along each of the twelve co-detection sightlines. The expected Galactic dispersion measure ($\text{DM}_{\text{MW}}$) is integrated to a distance of 30\,kpc. The pulse-broadening timescale ($\tau_{\text{MW}}$) and scintillation bandwidth ($\Delta\nu_{\text{MW}}$) are scaled from the NE2025 output at 1\,GHz to the center of the CHIME/FRB band (600\,MHz) and the DSA-110 band (1.4\,GHz) assuming Kolmogorov scaling ($\tau \propto \nu^{-4.4}$ and $\Delta\nu_d \propto \nu^{4.4}$).

One methodological asymmetry is deliberate. This Galactic prediction
uses a \emph{fixed} Kolmogorov frequency index ($\alpha=4.4$) to scale
$\tau_{\text{MW}}$ between bands, whereas the extragalactic scattering analysis
of Section~\ref{sec:jointfit} co-models the frequency scaling with the pulse
shape through the turbulence index $\beta$ rather than fixing $\alpha$. We keep
the fixed-$\alpha$ Kolmogorov value here as a fiducial for the \emph{Galactic}
term because NE2025 is itself calibrated on a Kolmogorov medium and because the
predicted Galactic broadening is sub-microsecond on every high-latitude
sightline (below), so the choice of Galactic scaling index cannot affect the
extragalactic attribution there. On the low-latitude sightlines, where
$\tau_{\text{MW}}$ is non-negligible, we carry this as an explicit systematic
rather than propagate the fitted $\beta$ posterior through a model (NE2025) that
does not itself expose one.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ne2025_mw}, the sightlines exhibit strongly
latitude-dependent behavior. High-latitude sightlines ($|b| > 30^\circ$)
show minimal Galactic contamination, with expected Milky Way DM
contributions $\text{DM}_{\text{MW}} \lesssim 41$\,pc\,cm$^{-3}$ and
sub-microsecond scattering broadening at CHIME frequencies
($\tau_{\text{MW}} \lesssim 0.1\,\mu$s). Low-latitude sightlines
($|b| < 15^\circ$) carry substantial Galactic contributions, with
$\text{DM}_{\text{MW}}$ reaching $\approx 97$\,pc\,cm$^{-3}$ and predicted
CHIME-band scattering timescales of $\tau_{\text{MW}} \gtrsim 30\,\mu$s,
which can dominate or mask the intrinsic extragalactic scattering.

The $\text{DM}_{\text{MW}}$ quoted in this section is the NE2025 \emph{disk}
(ISM) column alone. In the dispersion-measure budget of
Section~\ref{sec:dm} (Table~\ref{tab:budget}) we add the
$40\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$ Milky-Way halo prior to this disk term, so the
$\mathrm{DM_{MW}}$ column there ($74$--$137\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$) is larger
than the $\lesssim97\,\mathrm{pc\,cm^{-3}}$ disk-only values here by that fixed
halo offset; the two are consistent once the halo term is accounted for.

\subsection{Foreground-galaxy search}
\label{sec:obs-fg}

Before the intervening dispersion-measure and scattering terms can be computed, each
sightline must be searched for foreground galaxies and clusters, and every
candidate assigned a redshift and a foreground/background verdict. We run the
search in two stages---discovery followed by external-catalog
validation---and record the result in Table~\ref{tab:foreground}.

The discovery stage searches a wide cone around each localized burst. At the
sample's high declination ($+70^\circ$ to $+74^\circ$) only all-sky or northern
catalogs provide coverage. For galaxy-scale halos we query the DESI Legacy
Imaging Surveys DR8 North photometric-redshift catalog \citep[the random-forest
estimator of][]{Zhou2021}, GLADE+, and SDSS~DR12, supplemented by the
PS1-STRM photometric classifier \citep{Beck2021} for sources
without a Legacy match; for clusters we query the DESI Legacy/WISE cluster
catalog of \citet{WenHan2024}---which supplies $M_{500}$, $R_{500}$, and,
through its DESI members, a spectroscopic redshift---together with the all-sky
Planck Sunyaev--Zel'dovich (PSZ2) and X-ray (MCXC, MCXC-II) catalogs; at the
sample's declination every matched cluster comes from the optical catalog.
The wide discovery cone sets only the search completeness; galaxy retention is
then physical, keyed to each candidate's own circumgalactic scale rather than to
a single fixed impact parameter. For every galaxy candidate we estimate a
stellar mass---from Pan-STARRS1 $g-i$ colour \citep[the][relation]{Taylor2011}
where a clean optical detection exists, and otherwise from the WISE $W1$
luminosity \citep{Cluver2014}---convert it to a halo mass through the
stellar-to-halo-mass relation of \citet{Moster2013}, and assign a virial radius
$R_{\rm vir}=R_{200c}$ with the concentration--mass relation of
\citet{DuttonMaccio2014}. A galaxy is
retained as a circumgalactic intersector when the sightline passes within that
virial radius, $b \le R_{\rm vir}$. Because $R_{\rm vir}$ grows with halo mass,
this criterion admits genuine circumgalactic sightlines well beyond any fixed
circumgalactic radius. Among the confirmed foreground galaxy halos
the recovered $R_{\rm vir}$ span $\sim\!90$--$700\,\mathrm{kpc}$, and eleven of
the fourteen are pierced within $R_{\rm vir}$ (impact parameters
$b\approx102$--$237\,\mathrm{kpc}$, $b/R_{\rm vir}\approx0.15$--$0.88$); the three
exceptions---all toward FRB~20230307A---graze just outside their virial radii
($b\approx122$--$243\,\mathrm{kpc}$, $b/R_{\rm vir}\approx1.05$--$1.7$) and are
flagged accordingly. The full
non-refuted galaxy census reaches $b\approx60$--$280\,\mathrm{kpc}$. Cluster
retention is unchanged from the fixed-aperture scheme. Cluster cones are far
wider ($\sim\!5\,\mathrm{Mpc}$), and a cluster is kept when the sightline passes
within twice its virial radius ($\lesssim2\,R_{200}\approx3\,R_{500}$), so a
grazing outskirt is still recovered. Only crossings within $R_{500}$ enter the
dispersion-measure budget (Section~\ref{sec:foreground}), and the wider
retention aperture exists so the excluded near-misses can be shown explicitly
(Appendix~\ref{app:clusters}). The cluster masses and radii ($M_{500}$,
$R_{500}$) are optical-richness estimates from \citet{WenHan2024} rather than
X-ray or Sunyaev--Zel'dovich measurements, and the associated mass uncertainty
propagates into the intervening-column prior.

The virial-radius criterion is applied only \emph{after} redshift confirmation,
not in its place. Applied on its own, the geometric test would admit refuted
background galaxies whose sightlines happen to fall within an (often
mass-overestimated) virial radius. Stellar masses derived from WISE $W1$ without an optical
corroborating colour can be inflated by source blending or nuclear emission at
the sample's redshifts ($z\sim0.3$--0.6). Seven candidates have a WISE mass with
no usable Pan-STARRS1 colour to corroborate it; for five of these the WISE
estimate is implausibly large for an individual galaxy
($\log M_\star=11.7$--13.0), and the other two, though nominally lower, remain
uncorroborated. Their circumgalactic intersection is therefore formally
indeterminate. All seven are already inconclusive or refuted by the redshift
validation and none enters the confirmed budget; we report them as
mass-indeterminate rather than force a virial radius onto an uncorroborated mass.

Validation cross-matches every candidate, by coordinate, against three
independent redshift sources. These are the DESI Legacy Survey DR9 photometric
redshifts (the \citealt{Zhou2021} random-forest estimator, which supplies a
per-object redshift error and a morphological type), DESI~DR1 spectroscopic
redshifts, and the NASA/IPAC Extragalactic Database (NED). Matches use a $5''$ radius for
galaxies and a $90''$ radius for cluster centroids. The adopted redshift follows
a fixed priority---DESI spectroscopic, then Legacy spectroscopic, then Legacy
photometric (carrying its $1\sigma$ error), then NED---and a DESI spectrum
classified as a star is rejected outright, so a foreground stellar velocity can
never be adopted as a system redshift. A candidate is \emph{confirmed} when its
redshift (or photometric redshift plus $1\sigma$) lies below the host,
\emph{refuted} when it lies above, and \emph{inconclusive} when it falls within
$1\sigma$ of the host, when the host redshift is a placeholder, or when no
trustworthy redshift or galaxy classification exists (including the
PS1-STRM ``unsure'' class).

The census evaluated 35 candidate systems along the twelve sightlines.
Table~\ref{tab:foreground} lists the 28 that remain relevant to the budget:
15 confirmed foreground (14 galaxy halos and the single cluster whose
sightline passes within $R_{500}$) and 13 inconclusive---candidates the
available redshifts could not place on either side of the host, which
therefore bound the systematic uncertainty on each sightline's intervening
column. The remaining 7 candidates were refuted as background by the
validation stage and are omitted from the table, as are candidates rejected
outright as stars. A further 14 spectroscopically confirmed
foreground clusters lie along these sightlines at impact parameters beyond
$R_{500}$; outside the aperture used for the dispersion-measure budget, they
are likewise omitted---the \citet{WenHan2024} catalog reaches group scale
($M_{500}>0.47\times10^{14}\,M_\odot$), so several are low-mass systems. Because
only wide-field catalogs reach this declination, a sightline with no confirmed
system is unconstrained by survey depth rather than demonstrably foreground-free;
the budget of Section~\ref{sec:foreground} treats such sightlines accordingly.

Of the 15 confirmed foreground systems, nine carry a DESI or NED spectroscopic
(or the single cluster's DESI-member) redshift; the remaining six rest on a
DESI Legacy DR9 photometric redshift alone. Our confirmation criterion
($z_{\rm phot}+1\sigma < z_{\rm host}$) admits, per object, the $\sim16\%$
Gaussian probability that the true redshift exceeds $z_{\rm phot}+1\sigma$, plus
the few-percent catastrophic-outlier rate characteristic of Legacy DR9
photometric redshifts. Across the six photometric confirmations, this amounts to 
roughly one expected misclassification of a background galaxy as foreground.
Each of these six lies in the outer circumgalactic medium
($b\approx105$--$280\,\mathrm{kpc}$) and contributes only a small DM
column, so a misclassification would remove at most that object's column from
$\mathrm{DM_{int}}$; we therefore carry the summed photometric-confirmation
column as a one-sided systematic on $\mathrm{DM_{int}}$ for the affected
sightlines (Section~\ref{sec:foreground}) rather than treating those
confirmations as exact.

for this census as a function of redshift and signed impact parameter.
