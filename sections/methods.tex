\subsection{Band-restricted burst energies}
\label{sec:methods-energies}

For each sightline with a spectroscopic host redshift and a validated absolute
flux calibration in both bands at the burst position, we estimate the
isotropic-equivalent burst energy directly from the calibrated dynamic
spectra, without reference to any fitted spectral model.
Each band's fluence
is the per-channel on-pulse integral of the data: the per-channel
signal-to-noise spectrum, summed over the on-pulse window, is converted to
physical units with the radiometer conversion
$\sigma_{S,X}(\nu) = \mathrm{SEFD}_X(\nu)\,/\,[\sqrt{n_{\mathrm{pol}}\,\Delta\nu\,\Delta t}\,G_X(\nu)]$,
taking the system-equivalent flux density and primary-beam gain $G_X$ from the
DSA-110 \citep{Law2024} and CHIME/FRB \citep{CHIMEFRB2018,Michilli2021} flux
scales, and integrated over each instrument's observing band---CHIME over
$0.400$--$0.800\,\mathrm{GHz}$ and DSA over $1.311$--$1.499\,\mathrm{GHz}$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
E_{\mathrm{iso}} &= \frac{4\pi D_L^2(z)}{1+z}\left(I_C + I_D\right),\\
I_X &= \int_{\nu_1^{X}}^{\nu_2^{X}} \sigma_{S,X}(\nu)\,\Delta t
  \sum_{t \in \text{on-pulse}}
  \frac{d_X(\nu,t)}{\sigma_{N,X}(\nu)}\,d\nu,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:eiso}
\end{equation}
with $D_L(z)$ from the fiducial \textit{Planck}\,2018 cosmology
\citep{Planck2018}, the $(1+z)$ bandwidth k-correction applied, and
$1\,\mathrm{Jy\,ms\,Hz} = 10^{-29}\,\mathrm{J\,m^{-2}}$. Because the
integrand is the measured channel fluence, the estimate involves no
extrapolation and no spectral-shape parameter: in particular it is
insensitive to the steep, poorly constrained in-band spectral indices that a
narrow fractional band cannot pin down (a fitted-power-law cross-check
reproduces the per-band fluences within the calibration systematics). This
band-restricted integral constrains the energy released within the observed
spectral envelope: it avoids the large, model-dependent extrapolation that a
single power-law fit across the full $0.4$--$1.5\,\mathrm{GHz}$ span would
require, at the cost of not counting flux in the unobserved
$0.8$--$1.3\,\mathrm{GHz}$ gap.

Two properties of this definition must be stated prominently. First, the
observed bands map to \emph{per-burst} rest-frame intervals: CHIME's
$0.400$--$0.800\,\mathrm{GHz}$ samples the rest-frame band
$[0.400(1+z),\,0.800(1+z)]\,\mathrm{GHz}$ and DSA's
$1.311$--$1.499\,\mathrm{GHz}$ samples $[1.311(1+z),\,1.499(1+z)]\,\mathrm{GHz}$,
so the rest-frame window shifts from burst to burst. These band-restricted
energies are therefore \emph{not} comparable to literature energies computed
over a fixed rest-frame band, and cannot be placed on a standard FRB luminosity
function without a spectral-model extrapolation that we deliberately avoid here;
we report them as observed-band quantities only. Second, the single $(1+z)$ in
Eq.~\ref{eq:eiso} is the bandwidth (time-dilation) k-correction, which is common
to both bands and independent of any spectral shape, so the two-band sum is
internally consistent despite the single $(1+z)$ factor.

The quoted uncertainty combines, per band and in quadrature across bands, the
statistical fluence error with the absolute-scale systematic of the SEFD and
primary-beam model ($\sim$0.25\,dex CHIME, $\sim$0.20\,dex DSA); the DSA
scale is anchored to the published DSA-110 catalog fluences \citep{Law2024}
for the three co-detections that catalog covers (FRBs 20220207C, 20220310F,
20220506D, recovered at $1.27\times$, $2.15\times$, and $0.99\times$
respectively), and is model-trusted, without an external anchor, for the
remainder; no later published catalog permits widening this cross-check.
