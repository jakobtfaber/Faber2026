\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The per-sightline budget lets us ask, for each burst, whether its scattering can
be assigned to a specific screen. The clearest evidence for an extragalactic
screen is the diffractive-scintillation excess of Section~\ref{sec:scattering}:
on the mid-latitude sightlines where the Galactic floor is reliable, the measured
diffractive bandwidth lies $\sim$7--11$\times$ below the NE2025 prediction, and
never above it, so the dominant scattering screen is not the Milky Way. For the
prototype FRB~20221203A the three separated scintillation scales
(Figure~\ref{fig:scint_screens})---a Galactic floor far above and a
pulse-broadening screen far below the resolved diffractive scale---localize that
screen to the host or an intervening medium rather than the Galaxy.

Whether that screen is intervening or intrinsic to the host is, in principle,
decided by the foreground census. The two sightlines that pierce a foreground
galaxy's interior (FRB~20220310F, FRB~20230307A) carry both the
largest predicted intervening dispersion and the largest predicted intervening
pulse-broadening (Figure~\ref{fig:budget}, right), and are the natural candidates
for an intervening scattering screen. The high-amplitude scintillation-excess
sightlines, however, are not in general the galaxy-interior ones, so the present
evidence favors a host-dominated screen for the strongly scattered bursts, with
an intervening screen remaining viable only where a confirmed foreground galaxy
lies at small impact parameter. Discriminating the two requires the
measured-versus-predicted $\tau$ comparison that Figure~\ref{fig:budget} sets up
but cannot yet close until the per-sightline $\tau$ set is finalized into the
overlay; this is the test we identify as the principal remaining measurement for
the sample.

\subsection{Burst multiplicity as a systematic on the scattering index}
\label{sec:disc-alpha}

The result of Section~\ref{sec:results-alpha} is a cautionary systematic for any
single-component scattering index. A temporal sub-component omitted from the model
is partially absorbed into the scattering tail and biases $\alpha$---classically
high, as a steeper slope and a larger scattering time mimic the unmodeled flux,
but the bias can instead rail the fit against a prior bound when the multiplicity
is split across the two bands, as in FRB~20220310F where an unmodeled DSA-110
sub-pulse drives $\alpha$ to its \emph{lower} bound. Because $\alpha$ distinguishes
a Kolmogorov turbulent screen ($\alpha\simeq4.4$; \citealp{Rickett1990}) from a
shallower spectrum, an unrecognized companion can masquerade as anomalous
scattering---a regime in which measured pulse-broadening indices already scatter
well below the Kolmogorov value \citep{Bhat2004}. We verified that a favored
second component is not an artifact of the likelihood: its evidence gain is flat
across the gain-prior scale (so it is not prior-driven), reconstructing the
single-component residual reproduces the whiteness statistic that flagged the
burst, and the companion sits well inside the resolvable separation.

A second systematic is the assumed PBF. A band-dependent PBF
shape---formally favored for some bursts but unphysical for a single
sightline---can shift a marginal, poorly-constrained burst's $\alpha$ by several
tenths, while leaving the well-constrained sightlines unchanged at the $\le0.1$
level (Section~\ref{sec:jointfit}). Multiplicity and PBF must therefore both be
controlled before a per-sightline $\alpha$ is interpreted; the indices reported
here come from a uniform re-fit of the sample under a single physical (exponential)
PBF, modeling temporal multiplicity per band. For the marginal sightlines---those
whose posterior rails or reaches a prior bound under either choice---we quote no
index, treating them as unconstrained rather than anomalous.
