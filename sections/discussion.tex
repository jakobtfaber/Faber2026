\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The per-sightline budget lets us ask, for each burst, whether its scattering can
be assigned to a specific screen. The clearest evidence for an extragalactic
screen is the diffractive-scintillation excess of Section~\ref{sec:scattering}:
on the mid-latitude sightlines where the Galactic floor is reliable, the measured
diffractive bandwidth lies $\sim$7--11$\times$ below the NE2025 prediction, and
never above it, so the dominant scattering screen is not the Milky Way. For the
prototype FRB~20221203A the three separated scintillation scales
(Figure~\ref{fig:scint_screens})---a Galactic floor far above and a
pulse-broadening screen far below the resolved diffractive scale---localize that
screen to the host or an intervening medium rather than the Galaxy.

Whether that screen is intervening or intrinsic to the host is, in principle,
decided by the foreground census. The three sightlines that pierce a foreground
galaxy's interior (FRB~20220310F, FRB~20230307A, FRB~20230814A) carry both the
largest predicted intervening dispersion and the largest predicted intervening
pulse-broadening (Figure~\ref{fig:budget}, right), and are the natural candidates
for an intervening scattering screen. The high-amplitude scintillation-excess
sightlines, however, are not in general the galaxy-interior ones, so the present
evidence favors a host-dominated screen for the strongly scattered bursts, with
an intervening screen remaining viable only where a confirmed foreground galaxy
lies at small impact parameter. Discriminating the two requires the
measured-versus-predicted $\tau$ comparison that Figure~\ref{fig:budget} sets up
but cannot yet close, since no quality-passing $\tau$ is locked in; this is the
test we identify as the principal remaining measurement for the sample.

\subsection{Burst multiplicity as a systematic on the scattering index}
\label{sec:disc-alpha}

The result of Section~\ref{sec:results-alpha} is a cautionary systematic for any
single-component scattering index. A temporal sub-component omitted from the
model is partially absorbed into the scattering tail, biasing $\alpha$ high: in
the case we examined the bias is $\Delta\alpha\approx0.56$, many times the formal
uncertainty. Because $\alpha$ is the quantity that distinguishes a Kolmogorov
turbulent screen ($\alpha\simeq4.4$; \citealp{Rickett1990}) from a shallower
spectrum, an unrecognized companion can masquerade as anomalous,
sub-Kolmogorov scattering---a regime in which measured pulse-broadening indices
already scatter well below the Kolmogorov value
\citep{Bhat2004}. We verified
that the favored second component is not an artifact of the likelihood:
reconstructing the single-component residual reproduces the whiteness statistic
that flagged the burst, the evidence gain is free of the eigenvalue-conditioning
pathology that a spurious near-degenerate component would trigger, and the
companion sits well inside the resolvable separation. Even the two-component
model, however, does not fully whiten the residual---a sharp rising edge
survives, consistent with an intrinsic fast rise, a limitation of the
scattered-pulse kernel, or a third component---so the corrected $\alpha$ should
be read as better-conditioned rather than final. A systematic re-fit of the
flagged co-detections in our sample is underway.
